My Recent Works

Boeing Stress Analysis Tool

This was the first project I worked on during my 8-month co-op at Belcan. My team was assigned with the task of reworking an existing Boeing 777X stress analysis tool. The previous tool took 72 hours to run. Using a Graham scan algorithm, only the critical loadcases were considered. We also fixed a couple errors found in the previous tool. Our team finished this project ahead of the deadline and Boeing was very impressed with our work. Our tool was accepted by Boeing and is currently being used by stress engineers working on the 777X. The skills my team and I learned from this project are Excel VBA, Creative Problem-Solving, Software Development,Technical Writing and Boeing-Owned Stress Analysis Software.

Boeing Structural Repair Manuals

For my second project at Belcan, my team and I were assigned with the task of updating structural repair manuals (SRM) for multiple Boeing 777X wing structures. The SRMs consisted of both Repairable Damage Limits (RDL) and Allowable Damage Limits (ADL). Our team started with rerunning each analysis using the new set of loads. Each analysis provided our team with updated margins of safety. The ADL margins told us how much damage was allowed on each part and what types of damage each part could withstand. The RDL margins told us how repairing a damage part impacted its functionality and safety. After my team and I finished all required analyses and checked each other's work, we updated the structural repair manuals. Our team finished this project by the assigned deadline and Boeing was happy with our work. From this project, my team and I learned the skills of FEA (Patran, Nastran), CATIA V5, Enovia LCA, Technical Writing, Excel VBA, and Boeing-Owned Stress Analysis Software.

Boeing Centralized Repository

For my third and final Project at Belcan, my team and I were assigned with the task of creating a centralized repository containing fastener and element data for new Boeing 777X part iterations. From CATIA, we exported material, layup, thickness, shim, porosity and butt-splicing data. Extracting this data was straightforward, but organizing everything into a centralized repository by element ID and fastener ID was an immense challenge. This was accomplished through coordinate-matching in Excel VBA code. To avoid incorrect matching, we used a tolerance of 0.05 inches. This required us to use identical coordinates for each of the data extractions listed above. This requirement was extremely tedious, but we were able to overcome this challenge. Another complication occurred when Boeing stress engineers released updated geometry for two of the parts while we were nearing the end. This required us to redo our porosity and butt-splicing data for the impacted parts. Nearing the end of the project, we validated our data for every part by comparing our repository data to the CATIA data. After we ensured that our data was valid, we made our repository compatible with all Boeing stress analysis software (.csz, .bdf, .dta, etc.). From this project, my team and I learned the skills of Excel VBA, Software Development, Engineering Design Work, CATIA V5, Enovia LCA and File Compatibility.

Thermal Camera Calibration

This is a project I worked on at the University of Washington Autonomous Flight and Systems Laboratory. My lab partner were tasked with calibrating and overlaying thermal imagery on top of RGB imagery, a feat that has never been accomplished before. We started by designing a physical apparatus that could be used for camera calibration for both thermal and RGB imagery. We used the idea of checkerboard, as white and black squares have contrasting heat properties. However, since heat dissipates, getting the pattern to show up in the thermal camera was by far the hardest part of this project. Using aluminum squares on an acrylic board and applying a heat gun for about 15 seconds, we were able to replicate the checkerboard in the thermal camera. After we were able to finally replicate the pattern, we calibrated the thermal camera using OpenCV in Python. We were able to get the program to recognize the checkerboard pattern and properly draw lines. Knowing that our design was successful, future work would have consisted of using a laser cutter to get a more precise grid and producing more pictures to make our calibration more valid. While this project was fun and taught us a lot about camera architecture, our research professor decided it would be best for us to move onto another project and put this one on hold, given its difficulty and the fact that it has yet to be accomplished. Currently, my lab partner and I are working on developing trustworthy decision-making and control algorithms for autonomous systems. If time allows, we will resume our work on this project this upcoming school year. The skills my lab partner and I learned from this project are Software Development, Camera Architecture, OpenCV in Python and Camera Calibration.

Wilderness Search and Rescue

The implementation of autonomous UAVs significantly reduces the mission time and rescue team fatality rate. My team and I developed machine learning algorithms that taught our drones the difference between a human and a dog, deer, etc. These algorithms were implemented for both thermal and RGB cameras. Both cameras are equally important in search and rescue missions, as RGB imagery works best in plain sight and thermal imagery can spot humans through thick vegetation.
