Boeing Stress Analysis Tool

This was the first project I worked on during my 8-month co-op at Belcan. My team was assigned with the task of reworking an existing Boeing 777X stress analysis tool. The previous tool took 72 hours to run and lacked usability. Using a Graham scan algorithm, only the critical loadcases were considered. This reduced the total run time to under 1 hour while maintaining 99.9% accuracy to the original tool. We also fixed a couple errors found in the previous tool. Our team finished this project ahead of the assigned deadline and Boeing was very impressed with our work. Our tool was ultimately accepted by Boeing and is currently being used by stress engineers working on the 777X. I am subject to a non-disclosure agreement, so only non-proprietary information has been shared. If you would like to learn more about the work I did, feel free to contact my supervisor: jason.r.spohn@boeing.com

Boeing Structural Repair Manuals

For my second project at Belcan, my team and I were assigned with the task of updating structural repair manuals (SRMs) for multiple Boeing 777X wing structures. The SRMs consisted of both Repairable Damage Limits (RDL) and Allowable Damage Limits (ADL). Our team began by rerunning each analysis using the new set of loads and updated engineering geometry. Each analysis provided our team with updated margins of safety. The ADL margins told us how much damage was allowed on each part and what types of damage each part could withstand. The RDL margins told us how repairing a damaged part impacted its functionality and safety. After my team and I finished all required analyses and checked each other's work, we updated the structural repair manuals. Our team finished this project by the assigned deadline and Boeing was pleased with our work. I am subject to a non-disclosure agreement, so only non-proprietary information has been shared. If you would like to learn more about the work I did, feel free to contact my supervisor: jason.r.spohn@boeing.com

Boeing Centralized Repository

For my third and final Project at Belcan, my team and I were assigned with the task of creating a centralized repository containing fastener and element data for new Boeing 777X part iterations. From CATIA, we exported material, layup, thickness, shim, porosity and butt-splicing data. Extracting this data was simple, but organizing everything into a centralized repository by element ID and fastener ID was an immense challenge. Our team accomplished this through coordinate-matching in Excel VBA. To avoid incorrect matching, we used a tolerance of 0.05 inches. This required us to use identical coordinates for each of the data extractions listed above. This requirement was extremely tedious, but we were able to overcome this challenge. Towards the end of the project, we validated our data for every part by comparing the data in our repository to the CATIA data. After we ensured that our data was valid, we made our repository compatible with all Boeing stress analysis software (.csz, .bdf, .dta, etc.). I am subject to a non-disclosure agreement, so only non-proprietary information has been shared. If you would like to learn more about the work I did, feel free to contact my supervisor: jason.r.spohn@boeing.com

Thermal Camera Calibration

This is a project I worked on at the University of Washington Autonomous Flight and Systems Laboratory. My lab partner and I were tasked with calibrating and overlaying thermal imagery on top of RGB imagery, a feat that has never been accomplished before. We started by designing a physical apparatus that could be used for camera calibration for both thermal and RGB imagery. We used the idea of checkerboard, as white and black squares have contrasting heat properties. However, since heat dissipates, getting the pattern to show up in the thermal camera was by far the hardest part of this project. Using aluminum squares on an acrylic board and applying a heat gun for about 15 seconds, we were able to replicate the checkerboard in the thermal camera. After we were able to finally replicate the pattern, we calibrated the thermal camera using OpenCV in Python. We were able to get the program to recognize the checkerboard pattern and properly draw lines. Knowing that our design was successful, future work would have consisted of using a laser cutter to get a more precise grid and producing more pictures to make our calibration more valid. While this project was fun and taught us a lot about camera calibration and architecture, our research professor decided it would be best for us to move onto another project and put this one on hold, given its difficulty and the fact that it has yet to be accomplished.

SIGHT

SIGHT was my senior capstone project at the University of Washington. In collaboration with two fellow engineers, we developed an advanced computer system to aid first responders in wilderness search and rescue missions. Utilizing the YOLOv8 (You Only Look Once) object detection model and advanced image processing techniques, SIGHT provides accurate and efficient identification of people across diverse terrains. Our model was trained on thousands of annotated drone images from footage taken in differing conditions. Despite challenges due to occlusion and numerous environmental factors, SIGHT achieved a 63% model precision with an identification time of 105 ms. This displays the ability of SIGHT to balance performance and efficiency in critical real-world situations. SIGHT showcases my ability to collaborate with a team of engineers to solve a complex problem in a short period of time using machine learning models.
